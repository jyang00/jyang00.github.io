Air Quality in India: Understanding the Air Quality Index (AQI)¶

By Roshni Patel and Julie Yang¶

Introduction

When I visited my family in India for the first time in ten years, the first thing I noticed upon exiting the airport midday was the fact that the sky above me was no longer blue. In fact, it was a hazy gray, filled with particulate matter and other hazardous chemicals that Indian citizens breathed in every day. India, the second-largest country in the world by population, is no stranger to the issue of air pollution (video), and air quality within densely populated cities like Delhi and Ahmedabad (where my family is from) is continuing to grapple with the growing intensity of air pollution. The issue with the air quality is not only a concern for citizens of India but also for travelers and visitors of the country. Our project seeks to understand what factors influence air quality the most, and how air quality has changed over time.

About the Data

We will be using an open-sourced dataset from this repository on Kaggle.com which uses data that is directly collected by the Central Pollution Control Board of India. First, we downloaded the city_day csv file and saved it to a folder on our Google Drive so that we can open the dataset using Google Colab. We chose to use this dataset above the other datasets provided by this repository because we are only concerned about the air quality of cities, not the stations in India; we also did not need data to be as detailed by the hour, since we are comparing the air quality of cities themselves and not digging into a specific city.

As you can see, there are various factors that contribute to the air pollution level for a given day. It is important to be aware of the column for the Air Quality Index, otherwise known as AQI. The higher the AQI value, the more severe and dangerous the air quality is to human health. AQI is calculated from the various components listed in the table below. For those interested in understanding how AQI has been calculated, you can check out this resource.

Visualizing the Data

First, lets try to visualize the air quality for all the cities in India. This will help us get a better picture of the distribution of different severity levels of air quality. Red markers indicate the places that have the most hazardous air quality levels, yellow markers indicate moderate levels, and green markers indicate good air quality levels. These three levels of severity were specifically created by sorting the average AQI values for each city and forming three evenly divided groups.

Important Notes

When we were extracting the data for the AQI in 2019, we discovered that the cities Aizawl, Ernakulam, and Kochi only had AQI data for 2020; therefore, these three cities were excluded from our observation. The data in 2020 is not a good representation of the air quality per city in India due to the lockdown period (from March to May). As a result of the lockdown, there was likely less pollution from emissions due to transportation from cars, rickshaws, and motorcycles. Thus we chose to use the data from the year 2019, before the pandemic, to show the air quality levels of each city.

Choosing Cities

After looking at this graph, we decided to focus the rest of our exploration of the data on one city with good air quality (Amaravati), one city with moderate air quality (Kolkata), and one with severe air quality (Delhi). We decided on these cities after looking through the dataset, and seeing which had missing values for some of the components that are used for calculating air quality. We also searched for cities that had data that was available across multiple years so that we could see their air quality change over longer periods of time. These three cities that we chose were far apart from each other and thus, the air quality data in these cities will be independent of each other.

Which Factor Influences Air Quality the Most?

One of our main questions from this graph was understanding which components had the biggest impact on air quality. While outside research including the videos above has mentioned particulate matter and ozone being major factors in what makes the air quality especially dangerous for compromised groups like children and the elderly, we wanted to see if we could use a decision tree classification algorithm to categorize the air quality bucket correctly.

Choosing Classification

We settled upon the idea of doing classification for the AQI bucket because we felt that it would be more insightful than simply predicting the value of the air quality. Currently, the five options for AQI buckets are Good, Satisfactory, Moderate, Poor, Very Poor, and Severe. Rather than trying to do regression to find the specific air quality value, what we cared about understanding the most was the severity level of the air quality, which naturally led us to choose classification. Decision trees are fast, easy to understand, and require very little pre-preparation of the data beforehand, which is why we chose to use the decision tree algorithm. For those interested in exploring decision trees further, you can check out this resource on scikit-learn.

Using Decision Tree Classification to Predict AQI Bucket

The code below takes one of the many components that are used to predict air quality (PM2.5, PM10, NO, NO2, NOx, CO, SO2, O3, Benzene, Toluene, Xylene) and runs the decision tree classifier on the data, in order to see which component has the highest accuracy of classification for the AQI bucket. Below are the results of the decision tree classifier and the confusion matrix for each test.

from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split

from sklearn.model_selection import cross_validate

from sklearn.metrics import confusion_matrix
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score
from sklearn import preprocessing
from sklearn import utils

#These are the components or factors that are considered in calculating AQI value
components = ['PM2.5','PM10','NO', 'NO2', 'NOx', 'CO', 'SO2','O3', 'Benzene', 'Toluene', 'Xylene']

#These are the 3 cities we chose to work with: Good AQI = Amaravati, Moderate AQI = Kolkata, Severe AQI = Delhi
cities = ["Delhi", "Kolkata", "Amaravati"]

#Takes each component, and runs the decision tree classifier on them, attempting
#to match the component to the correct AQI Bucket value for the day
def classificationPerComponent(df, component):
  y=df[['AQI_Bucket']]
  X=df[[component]]

  #Uses the scikit library to train the data, splitting into 20% testing 80% training
  X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.2, random_state=0)

  #Fitting the data to the decision tree classifier
  classifier = DecisionTreeClassifier(random_state=0)
  classifier.fit(X_train, y_train)

  #Predicting using the classifer
  y_pred = classifier.predict(X_test)

  #Creating the confusion matrix and printing the accuracy score
  cm = confusion_matrix(y_test, y_pred)
  print(cm, 'Accuracy for ' + str(component) + ": " + str(accuracy_score(y_test, y_pred)), "\n")

#This section of code will make scatter plots for AQI and PM2.5 over the years of 2018-2020 in the 3 cities above

#Labels created for the ticks in the graph. Contains all 12 months of the years 2018-2020.
month_year_label = ["1-2018", "2-2018", "3-2018", "4-2018", "5-2018", "6-2018", "7-2018", "8-2018", "9-2018", "10-2018", "11-2018", "12-2018",
                    "1-2019", "2-2019", "3-2019", "4-2019", "5-2019", "6-2019", "7-2019", "8-2019", "9-2019", "10-2019", "11-2019", "12-2019",
                    "1-2020", "2-2020", "3-2020", "4-2020", "5-2020", "6-2020", "7-2020", "8-2020", "9-2020", "10-2020", "11-2020", "12-2020"]

#A function to create a scatter plot for each measure or component (e.g. AQI, PM2.5) that compares values for 3 different cities
def plot_per_measure(measure):
  #For each of the 3 cities
  for city in cities:
    #A dictionary that stores all months in 2018-2020 with a total sum of the measure and number of values that were read
    measure_months = {key: {"total":0, "count":0} for key in month_year_label}

    #Extracts all rows that had the current city
    city_df = city_day[(city_day['City'] == city)]

    #For each row, ind = row number
    for ind in city_df.index:
      #Converts the string date from the current row into a datetime
      date = pd.to_datetime(city_df["Date"][ind], format="%Y-%m-%d")
      #Creates a string of month-year (like how the labels are formatted)
      month_year_str = str(date.month) + "-" + str(date.year)

      #If the value of the measure is not missing and the date for the current row is in 2018 or later
      if not np.isnan(city_df[measure][ind]) and date.year >= 2018:
        #Increment the measure's total by the value of the measure and count by 1 for the corresponding month-year
        measure_months[month_year_str]["total"] = measure_months[month_year_str]["total"] + city_df[measure][ind]
        measure_months[month_year_str]["count"] = measure_months[month_year_str]["count"] + 1
    #Calculates the averages of the measure for each month in 2018-2020
    #If the count was 0, meaning no data for the measure in that month, then nan is used instead
    avgs = map(lambda val: val["total"]/val["count"] if val["count"] != 0 else np.nan, measure_months.values())

    #Plots the scatter plot of measure over time for the current city
    plt.scatter(month_year_label, list(avgs), label=city)

Using Regression to Predict AQI

Next, we wanted to create a regression model for AQI over time, and use a violin plot to show the range of residual values. import statsmodels.api as sm
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

#A function to calculate the regression model with an interaction term and displays the summary of the model with its p-values for the given city
def interactionForCity(df, city):
  #Adds the interaction between year and PM2.5 value to predict AQI
  df['Year'] = pd.DatetimeIndex(df['Date']).year 
  df=df[df['Year']>=2018]
  year_pm25df = df[['Year','PM2.5']]
  poly = PolynomialFeatures(interaction_only=True,include_bias = False)
  x_inter=poly.fit_transform(year_pm25df)

  #Fits the regression with the transformed x values
  reg = LinearRegression()
  reg.fit(x_inter, df[['AQI']])

  #Prints the coefficients and intercept in the form of an equation
  print("New regression model using Year AND PM2.5 as the interaction term for the City: " + str(city))

  print('-------------------------------------------------------------------------')

  #Fit the model using the AQI actual values and the transformed x values
  model = sm.OLS(df[['AQI']], x_inter).fit()

  print("\nPVALUES for City: " + str(city))
  #Prints p value
  print(model.pvalues)

  print('\n-------------------------------------------------------------------------')
